**I drove to Berkeley from a city no one could place on a map,** over hills, through fog. Orientation week started and I already found myself boundlessly swimming through close-knit pods of freshmen who could, at least, feign purpose. I waded aimlessly. I thought my means necessitated this. 

The University tossed commuters in any bucket that wasn't overflowing. As my orientation group bled members to the frats and the sororities, I remained at the bottom of the bucket. I clung onto hope by looking forward towards an experience I'd been assured would be life-changing and define the next four years of my life. That experience wasn't my education.

We met at Smokehouse. I was worried sick because I missed the band's fall training program, but as I settled in, an overwhelming sense of cohesion and camraderie came over me. Cal Band welcomed me with open arms. All of a sudden, I had people to meet in the dining halls. I got invited to my first college party. I had spent the last four years in a cell, filing away at the bars until they finally dropped at graduation, weariness compounding. 

**The band hall had a strangley familiar air to it.** When you keep a bunch of instruments in the same room, I think the same type of scent tends to build, regardless of the composition of the room itself. I saw one of the only places that gave me refuge through middle and high school through a kaleidoscope. Signs sporting a rainbow of school colors, logos, and NCAA team names lined the walls, each more conspicuous than the last, each more suspiciously sourced. Each, a trophy of some institution's misplaced trust in our organization. Having spent years with my head down, swimming under radar pings, the promise that I, too, would be able to add a trophy to the walls 

That Friday, I got in a minor car accident, throwing a rock through the window that was my manic happiness. I saw the shards of this new glass effigy of myself tumble to the ground.
